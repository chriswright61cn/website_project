1.1: The purpose of IT in modern business

IT now is integrated at all levels of business. Large businesses once used large computers dedicated to single uses such as payroll then the introduction of personal computers brought business technology and cost savings to small businesses. Word processors, spreadsheets and databases generate files and data that can be organised and searched and are time efficient compared to paper records.

Communication tools have evolved with email and messaging systems and the world wide web has made any company’s website the first point of contact and made sales available globally via ecommerce.

1.2: Skills and resources needed to complete digital projects successfully

The starting point of a digital project is to identify the problem that the project is addressing and it’s possible solutions. The goals should be clearly defined and once method has been chosen to achieve these you can decide on the work that needs to be done. This solution should be achievable within the time available. Unrealistic goals will probably result in failure.

Tasks should be broken down into small manageable chunks that can be easily tracked with project management software such as Trello. This should show what needs to be done once the reasons why these tasks need to be done and how to do them has been decided on.

Deciding when these tasks should be done and prioritising tasks effectively then becomes important. There is a difference between important and urgent and there is no point prioritising everything as urgent


1.3: Plan and carry out tasks using IT

The website involves some basic planning. The main content is text which needs to be written. Any images or media need to be created or acquired but there are no extra elements to consider such as forms, maps or image slideshows.

This website will consist of a number of sections of text each contained in a div which will be identified in a heading. These sections will be split into several linked pages. The number of individual pages for the content should be decided on and set up. A navigation system to link the pages should be added. Internal jump links should be added to allow easy access to information within a page and the links addedThe sections are organised by the page where they will appear and the status of content creation and placement tracked on Trello.

1.4: Risks to digital projects

Projects can fail for many reasons. If the reason for the project is unclear or loosely defined it can be difficult to find the best way to reach a final product. Improper planning leaves a project with no clear direction. Feature creep at an early stage can lead to a lack of focus on the essentials

Trying to do too much at once will ensure that nothing is finished. At the very least a project should have an achievable minimum viable product to aim for. This can then be enhanced progressively at a later date. This will ensure that the project is working in the shortest time. No product is ever perfect or fully finished and improvements and changes will always be required.

1.5: Selecting and using IT systems and software

There are many ways of approaching any digital project with a plethora of tools available for any task. A technology stack is a combination of libraries or tools, languages, and frameworks that developers can choose from and these choices can be overwhelming with new technologies constantly appearing.

Planning a web project will depend on the size of a project. A simple website could consist of static linked web-pages. For quick projects that have to be working quickly, a content management system like such as Wordpress and a ready made style-theme may be the most suitable option.

As websites become more complex then different front end and back end (server) systems may become appropriate.

1.7: Legal guidelines & constraints

A number of legal requirements can impact projects from privacy with gathered data, online sales which are subject to the same laws as other sales transaction.Any financial transactions should be properly protected with encryption . Trademark and copyright laws have to be followed for both written content as well as photography and graphics. Attribution for any work that was not originally should be made where required if using creative commons images etc.

Our digital lives revolve around data. Names and addresses, IP addresses, credit card information, images and even genetic and biometric information as well as our interactions on the web and social media are tracked, stored and analysed .This personal data can be collected and correlated to give a digital profile by governments, retailers and social media platforms among others.

The General Data Protection Regulation is central to Europe's digital privacy legislation with laws about personal data, privacy and consent. With GDPR, organisations have to ensure that personal data is gathered legally under strict conditions including explicit op-ins to give consent to do this. They have to protect this information, and give access to people’s own personal data, show how it is being used and delete it if requested.
